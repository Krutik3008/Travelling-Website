Gomateshwara

Gomateshwara, also known as Bahubali, is a legendary figure and an important Jain pilgrimage site located in the town of Shravanabelagola in the Indian state of Karnataka. The Gomateshwara statue is considered to be one of the largest monolithic statues in the world, and is widely regarded as a masterpiece of Jain art and architecture.

History

The Gomateshwara statue was commissioned by the Jain ruler Chavundaraya and was carved out of a single block of granite in the 10th century AD. The statue is 57 feet tall and is considered to be one of the most magnificent examples of Jain art and architecture. The statue is also an important symbol of non-violence and spiritualism in Jainism.

Symbolism

The Gomateshwara statue is an important symbol of Jainism. The statue represents the Jain ideal of ahimsa, or non-violence, as Gomateshwara is shown standing motionless and peaceful, with vines growing around his legs and arms. The statue also represents the Jain concept of detachment from the material world, as Gomateshwara is depicted standing naked without any worldly possessions.

Visiting

The Gomateshwara statue is open to visitors every day from sunrise to sunset. Visitors are required to climb 614 steps to reach the top of the hill where the statue is located. Along the way, visitors can see a number of other Jain temples and shrines. The best time to visit the Gomateshwara statue is during the Mahamastakabhisheka festival, which is held once every 12 years.
